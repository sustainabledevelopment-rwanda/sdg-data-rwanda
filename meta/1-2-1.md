source_other_info_1: Poverty in Rwanda is measured using the EICV survey series through a monetary approach based on real household consumption per adult equivalent, adjusted for household composition and price differences. While the core methodology—consumption aggregation, equivalence scaling, and deflation—remains consistent, poverty lines have evolved across three series to reflect changing conditions and improved methods: in the 2001 series, the total and extreme poverty lines were 64,000 and 45,000 RWF per adult equivalent per year; in the 2014 series, they increased to 159,375 and 105,064 RWF; and in the 2024 series (EICV7), following major methodological improvements, they rose to 560,127 and 356,432 RWF. These revisions enhance measurement accuracy but limit strict comparability across periods.

computation_definitions: In Rwanda, the poverty line is computed using a cost-of-basic-needs approach that estimates the minimum consumption required to meet essential food and non-food needs. The food (extreme) poverty line reflects the cost of achieving a minimum caloric intake—revised from 2,500 to 2,400 kcal in the latest series—based on the consumption patterns of a reference group (now the second quintile), using a semi-normative food basket. The total poverty line is then derived by adjusting this food line to include basic non-food needs, using the typical share of food expenditure among households near the poverty threshold. These poverty lines underpin key indicators such as the headcount poverty rate and poverty gap, which measure the incidence and depth of poverty.
national_indicator_description: The poverty line in Rwanda is defined as the threshold of annual household consumption per adult equivalent below which an individual is considered poor, based on a cost-of-basic-needs approach. Total household consumption is aggregated from expenditures on food (including home-produced consumption), non-food goods and services, health, education, housing and utilities, in-kind income, and the use value of durable goods, then adjusted using adult equivalence scales that reflect household size and composition. The food (extreme) poverty line is derived from the cost of a minimum consumption basket that meets required caloric needs—revised from 2,500 to 2,400 kcal per adult equivalent per day in the latest EICV7—based on the consumption patterns of a reference group (now the second quintile). The total poverty line is obtained by scaling up the food poverty line to include essential non-food needs, using the observed share of food expenditure among households near the poverty threshold.

The data for this indicator are from the Integrated Household Living Conditions Survey, commonly known by its French acronym EICV (Enquête Intégrale sur les Conditions de Vie des Ménages), is a large-scale national survey designed and conducted by the National Institute of Statistics of Rwanda (NISR). It serves as a primary source of socio-economic information used to monitor the living conditions of Rwandan households and assess the progress of national and global development strategies. The central goal of the EICV series is to measure poverty and provide a detailed profile of the poor to help government agencies and partners identify vulnerable populations.
